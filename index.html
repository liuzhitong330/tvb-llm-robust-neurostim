Pipeline Overview
Three stages from raw literature to a validated stimulation strategy.

Drug-resistant epilepsy affects 15 million people worldwide. For many of them, the only remaining option is brain stimulation — surgically implanted devices that deliver electrical pulses to suppress seizures. But the problem is fundamental: no one knows which parameters to use. Stimulation frequency, amplitude, and target location are currently tuned by trial and error, one patient at a time, over months of clinical follow-up.

This project asks: can we use AI to do better?

The approach is a three-stage pipeline. First, we systematically read the scientific literature to find what the field says is missing — an automated pipeline that extracts research gaps from 136 papers and ranks them. The top finding: stimulation strategies optimized for one patient often fail when applied to another. Second, we build digital twins of the epileptic brain using The Virtual Brain — a computational simulator that models how seizures propagate across 76 brain regions connected by real white matter pathways. Think of it as a flight simulator for the brain: test interventions virtually, before touching a real patient. Third, we use a large language model as an optimization engine — not to generate text, but to reason over simulation results and propose better stimulation parameters, targeting the worst-case patient rather than the average.

The result is a proof-of-concept that goes from reading papers, to building a brain model, to finding stimulation parameters that work across a distribution of patients — not just one.

Literature Mining

10 PubMed queries → 136 deduplicated papers. Claude Haiku extracts structured gaps from every abstract. Claude Opus scores 30 thematic clusters on novelty, TVB feasibility, and clinical impact.

We performed a structured four-step literature mining pipeline:

  1. Query generation — 10 targeted PubMed queries spanning virtual brain modeling, closed-loop stimulation, and patient-specific epilepsy treatment
  2. Retrieval + deduplication — 136 papers after exact DOI and title matching across query results
  3. Gap extraction — 1,080 LLM-based extraction tasks (~8 extraction passes per abstract to capture multiple gap types) using a structured prompt (Claude Haiku). Each abstract was processed to extract: stated limitations, implicit methodological constraints, and unresolved clinical translation gaps
  4. Clustering + ranking — gaps were grouped into ~30 thematic clusters via embedding-based semantic similarity, then scored by Claude Opus on three axes: Novelty (how unlikely the gap is to be explicitly addressed in existing literature), TVB Feasibility (whether the question can be instantiated within TVB simulations), and Clinical Impact (relevance to real-world epilepsy treatment). Final score = equal-weighted sum (max 30) (each axis scored 1–10).

The top-ranked gap scored 25/30 under this rubric — aligned with the selected research direction. This score is an auxiliary consistency signal, not a proof of correctness.

This pipeline is fully automated and does not include domain-expert validation. Outputs should be treated as hypothesis generation rather than verified insights.

TVB Digital Twin

The Virtual Brain Epileptor model instantiated for 5 virtual patients with sampled variability in epileptogenicity (x₀) and inter-regional coupling (K). Each parameter set produces a reward signal measuring seizure suppression.

The Epileptor model captures seizure dynamics through six coupled ODEs with a dual-timescale structure: fast variables (x₁, y₁) represent rapid ictal oscillations, while slow variable z governs the gradual buildup toward seizure onset and eventual self-termination. The key clinical parameter is x₀, the epileptogenicity — values near −1.6 push tissue toward the seizure bifurcation threshold, while −3.0 represents well-controlled tissue far from seizure. Five virtual patients are generated by sampling inter-regional coupling K ± 0.003 steps around a baseline, modelling the biological variability a clinician encounters across a real cohort. Each simulation runs for 1,000 time steps at dt = 0.05 ms; reward is defined as −var(x₁), a model-agnostic proxy for seizure intensity that requires no hand-crafted features. TVB was chosen for its patient-specific whole-brain connectivity framework, its rigorous validation against clinical EEG, and its clean Python API that integrates directly into automated optimisation loops.

In practice, brain stimulation parameters (e.g., pulse amplitude, frequency, and target region) perturb the effective excitability and coupling of neural populations. In this prototype, we abstract this effect and operate directly on the resulting model parameters (x₀, K) — treating them as a compact summary of stimulation-induced changes in network dynamics rather than clinically prescribed values.

The Epileptor Model

The Epileptor is a phenomenological neural mass model developed by Jirsa et al. (2014) specifically to capture the essential dynamics of epileptic seizures. It consists of six coupled ODEs operating across two timescales:

Fast subsystem — ictal oscillations
dx₁/dt = y₁ − f₁(x₁, x₂) − z + I₁ dy₁/dt = 1 − 5x₁² − y₁
Slow permittivity variable
dz/dt = (1/τ₀)(4(x₁ − x₀) − z)
Fast subsystem 2 — interictal spikes
dx₂/dt = −y₂ + x₂ − x₂³ + I₂ + 2u − 0.3(z − 3.5) dy₂/dt = (1/τ₂)(−y₂ + f₂(x₂))
Coupling variable
du/dt = −γ(u − 0.1x₁)

The parameter x₀ (epileptogenicity) sits at the heart of the model. Mathematically, x₀ shifts the slow nullcline and determines whether a brain region sits in a stable fixed point (healthy, x₀ < −2.05) or undergoes limit-cycle oscillations (seizing, x₀ > −2.05). This bifurcation boundary is the computational analog of the clinical concept of an epileptogenic zone.

The 76-Region Connectome

TVB uses a parcellated whole-brain structural connectome derived from diffusion tensor imaging (DTI) tractography of healthy human subjects, parcellated into 76 cortical and subcortical regions using the Desikan–Killiany atlas. Each region runs its own Epileptor instance. Inter-regional coupling follows a linear scheme:

region i receives: K × C_ij × x₁_j

where Cij is the normalized fiber tract density between regions i and j. Seizure activity can therefore propagate across anatomical white matter pathways — just as it does in real patients. The coupling strength K controls how strongly connected regions influence each other, making it a proxy for overall network excitability.

From Simulation to Reward

Each simulation produces a (1000 × 76) matrix of x₁ values — the primary seizure activity variable across all 76 brain regions over time. The reward signal is defined as:

reward = −mean( var(x₁, axis=time) )

This takes the temporal variance of x₁ in each region (high during seizure, low during quiescence), averages across all 76 regions, and negates so that higher reward = less seizure activity. This is a model-agnostic proxy that requires no hand-crafted seizure detection — the optimizer naturally learns to suppress oscillations across the whole brain.

Minimax Optimization

A DBS or RNS device is implanted in one specific patient — parameters that achieve excellent average reward but leave any individual in uncontrolled seizure activity are clinically unacceptable. The optimisation objective is therefore the minimax reward: the minimum across all five virtual patients, not the mean. Two algorithmic approaches were evaluated and compared below.

We frame the problem as a minimax optimization under sampled patient variability — optimizing for worst-case performance rather than claiming cross-patient consistency. This is evaluated on a small cohort of 5 virtual patients with limited perturbations (K ±0.003); robustness claims are restricted to this sampled variability regime.

v1 · LLM-Guided RL Loop

Claude Opus acts as optimization oracle: proposes one (x₀, K) set per iteration, observes worst-case reward across all 5 virtual patients, then reasons over the full history to improve. 8 iterations total.

x₀ = −2.1 K = 0.0165 worst-case −0.3182 +40% vs baseline
v2 · AlphaEvolve-Inspired Evolution ✦ upgraded

Maintains a population of 10 candidate parameter sets. Each generation: evaluate all 10 on 5 virtual patients, select the top-5 elites, then the LLM reasons jointly over the elite set and proposes 5 diverse mutations — fine-tune near an elite, recombine two elites, execute a bold jump, or explicitly escape a local optimum. 5 generations = 50 TVB simulations. Population diversity pays dividends in higher-dimensional search: by Generation 3 the best candidate had already surpassed the v1 optimum.

x₀ = −2.08 K = 0.0290 worst-case −0.3176 +39.9% vs baseline

❝ A failure of external intervention?

Experiments reveal that direct tuning of epileptogenicity (x₀) yields a 20× higher suppression rate than external periodic stimulation (Iext). This points to a critical insight: epilepsy in the Epileptor model is a state-level bifurcation phenomenon. Simply superimposing a periodic signal is like trying to stop a tidal wave with a fan — a truly effective treatment must fundamentally shift the underlying neural excitability, not just perturb it from outside.

Why Is External Stimulation So Hard?
  • Limit cycle entrainment The Epileptor generates strong limit cycle oscillations. A simple sinusoidal Iext cannot break the attractor — it can only perturb it transiently.
  • Phase-resetting needed Effective closed-loop stimulation likely requires synchronizing pulses to seizure phase (phase-resetting control), not open-loop sinusoids.
  • Long-term plasticity Real DBS may work by gradually reshaping synaptic weights — indirectly shifting x₀ or K over weeks — not by acute signal superposition. This mechanism is absent from the current model.
Generalization & Robustness
Evaluating whether optimized parameters hold up on unseen patients and under wider variability.

To assess whether the optimized parameters generalize beyond the training cohort, we evaluated them on 8 unseen virtual patients (different random seed, same variability regime). We also tested robustness under wider patient variability and ran a stress test across 30 randomly sampled patients.

Train worst-case: −0.2847 → Test worst-case: −0.3346. The direction is consistent, indicating the solution generalizes to newly sampled patients from the same perturbation distribution — not just the 5 it was optimized on. We also observe stable performance across sampled patients rather than improvements driven by a single extreme case — the optimized parameters consistently outperform baseline across all sampled individuals, not only at the worst-case boundary.

Robustness degrades gracefully as variability increases: optimized worst-case remains substantially better than baseline across narrow (±0.003), medium (±0.005), and wide (±0.010) coupling perturbation regimes.

Stress test across 30 patients confirms distributional advantage: optimized median = −0.299 (vs baseline −0.442), std = 0.037 (vs 0.040), worst-case = −0.405 (vs −0.518).

These results should be interpreted as a proof-of-concept for robustness-oriented optimization, rather than a definitive demonstration of cross-patient consistency. Performance is expected to degrade as patient variability increases beyond the sampled regime.
